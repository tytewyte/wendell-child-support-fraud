APPENDIX D
ESSENTIAL LEGAL RESOURCES

If you're going to represent yourself in family court or child support proceedings, you need the right tools. This appendix provides you with essential legal resources that will help you understand the law, prepare your case, and fight effectively.

I've organized these resources into categories: books, websites, organizations, and tools. Use them. Study them. Master them.

Knowledge is power. And in family court, knowledge can mean the difference between victory and defeat.

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

ESSENTIAL BOOKS

1. REPRESENT YOURSELF IN COURT: PREPARE & TRY A WINNING CIVIL CASE
   By Paul Bergman, J.D. & Sara J. Berman
   Publisher: Nolo Press (10th Edition, 2019)

This is the single most important book you can read if you're representing yourself in court.

WHAT IT COVERS:
• How to file a lawsuit and respond to one
• Pretrial procedures and discovery
• How to investigate your case
• Settlement strategies
• Pretrial motions
• How to prove your case at trial
• Selecting a judge or jury
• Opening statements
• Direct examination of witnesses
• Cross-examination techniques
• Closing arguments
• Rules of evidence
• How to present exhibits
• Making and responding to objections
• Organizing a trial notebook
• Expert witnesses

WHY YOU NEED IT:
This book teaches you everything you need to know about courtroom procedure. It's written in plain English by experienced attorneys who understand that self-represented litigants need practical, step-by-step guidance.

When I was preparing for my hearings, I wished I had this book. It would have taught me how to properly present my evidence, how to cross-examine witnesses, how to make objections, and how to structure my arguments.

If you're going to court, read this book first. Then read it again. Then keep it with you as a reference throughout your case.

HOW TO USE IT WITH THIS BOOK:
• My book (Fighting Back) gives you the strategy and motivation
• Represent Yourself in Court gives you the tactics and procedures
• Together, they make you a formidable pro se litigant

WHERE TO GET IT:
• Nolo.com (official publisher)
• Amazon
• Barnes & Noble
• Your local library (check it out for free)

COST: Approximately $45-50 (worth every penny)

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

2. THE CRIMINAL LAW HANDBOOK: KNOW YOUR RIGHTS, SURVIVE THE SYSTEM
   By Paul Bergman, J.D. & Sara J. Berman
   Publisher: Nolo Press

If your child support case involves contempt charges (which can result in jail time), you need to understand criminal procedure. This book explains your constitutional rights, how criminal cases work, and how to defend yourself.

WHY IT MATTERS:
Child support contempt proceedings are quasi-criminal. You can be jailed for non-payment. You have the right to due process, the right to confront witnesses, and the right to present evidence. This book explains those rights.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

3. NOLO'S DEPOSITION HANDBOOK
   By Paul Bergman, J.D. & Albert Moore, J.D.
   Publisher: Nolo Press

If your case involves depositions (sworn testimony taken before trial), this book teaches you how to prepare for and conduct depositions effectively.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

4. YOUR STATE'S FAMILY LAW HANDBOOK

Most states have a family law handbook published by the state bar association or a legal publisher. These handbooks explain your state's specific laws, procedures, and forms.

For Louisiana residents:
• Louisiana Family Law Handbook (available through Louisiana State Bar Association)
• Louisiana Code of Civil Procedure (available online for free)
• Louisiana Revised Statutes (available online for free)

Find your state's handbook and study it. Know your state's laws better than your opponent does.

ESSENTIAL TOOLS

1. EVIDENCE ORGANIZATION SYSTEM

You need a system to organize your evidence. I recommend:

PHYSICAL BINDER:
• 3-ring binder with dividers
• Sections for: pleadings, discovery, evidence, correspondence, notes
• Bring to every hearing

DIGITAL SYSTEM:
• Scan all documents
• Organize in folders on your computer
• Back up to cloud storage (Google Drive, Dropbox, etc.)
• Easy to search and share

WHY BOTH:
Physical binder for court. Digital system for backup and easy access.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

2. TIMELINE CREATION TOOL

Create a timeline of all important events in your case. Include:
• Dates of court orders
• Dates of payments
• Dates of communications
• Dates of violations

TOOLS:
• Excel or Google Sheets (simple and effective)
• Timeline software (if you want something fancy)
• Hand-drawn timeline (if that works for you)

WHY IT MATTERS:
A clear timeline helps you see patterns, identify contradictions, and present your case logically.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

3. DOCUMENT COMPARISON TOOL

If you have contradictory documents (like I did with five audits), you need a way to compare them side-by-side.

TOOLS:
• Excel or Google Sheets (create comparison chart)
• Microsoft Word (track changes feature)
• PDF comparison software

USE IT FOR: Creating exhibits that show contradictions clearly and visually.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

4. AUDIO RECORDING DEVICE

Check your state's laws on recording conversations. If it's legal in your state, record all phone calls and in-person conversations related to your case.

WHY IT MATTERS:
People lie. Recordings don't. If someone makes a statement and later denies it, you have proof.

IMPORTANT: Know your state's recording laws. Some states require two-party consent (both parties must know they're being recorded). Other states only require one-party consent (you can record without telling the other person).

Louisiana is a one-party consent state, meaning you can record conversations you're part of without telling the other person.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

5. CASE MANAGEMENT NOTEBOOK

Keep a notebook (physical or digital) where you track:
• Deadlines
• Court dates
• Tasks to complete
• Questions to ask
• Ideas for arguments
• Notes from research

WHY IT MATTERS:
Family court cases are complex and long. You'll forget things if you don't write them down. A case management notebook keeps you organized and on track.

A WORD ABOUT HIRING AN ATTORNEY

I've written this book assuming you're representing yourself (pro se). But that doesn't mean you should never hire an attorney.

WHEN TO CONSIDER HIRING AN ATTORNEY:
• Your case is extremely complex
• You're facing serious consequences (jail time, loss of custody)
• You can afford it
• You've found an attorney you trust

UNBUNDLED LEGAL SERVICES:
If you can't afford full representation, consider "unbundled" or "limited scope" representation. This means you hire an attorney for specific tasks (like drafting a motion or representing you at one hearing) rather than for the entire case.

Many attorneys offer unbundled services at reduced rates. Ask about it.

WHAT IF YOU CAN'T AFFORD AN ATTORNEY:
• Apply for legal aid (if you qualify based on income)
• Look for pro bono attorneys (free representation)
• Use law school clinics (law students supervised by professors)
• Represent yourself using the resources in this appendix

Remember: representing yourself is not ideal, but it's better than not fighting at all. Use these resources, educate yourself, and fight with everything you have.
