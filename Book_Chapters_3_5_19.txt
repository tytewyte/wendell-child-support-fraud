THE REVENUE MODEL

Family court, especially child support enforcement, operates on a federal funding model that most people don't know exists.

It's called Title IV-D of the Social Security Act. Under this program, states receive federal funding based on how much child support they collect. The more they collect, the more federal money they get.

Sounds reasonable, right? Incentivize states to collect child support for children who need it.

But here's the problem: The incentive isn't based on accuracy. It's based on volume.

States don't get more money for collecting the *correct* amount of child support. They get more money for collecting *more* child support. Period.

So what happens? States maximize arrears. They inflate balances. They fabricate debts. Because every dollar they collect—whether it's legitimately owed or not—brings in federal matching funds.

In my case, Louisiana claimed I owed over $32,000. Even if half of that was fraudulent (and it was), they still got federal funding based on the full amount. They had no incentive to verify the debt. They had every incentive to maximize it.

This is why DCFS sent me five different audits. They weren't trying to find the truth. They were trying to find the highest number they could justify.

This is why Charlotte's messy, handwritten affidavit was accepted without question. The hearing officer didn't care if it was accurate. She cared that it supported a large arrears balance, which meant more federal funding for Louisiana.

This is why my dismissal document was ignored. Dismissing $10,150 would reduce the arrears balance, which would reduce federal funding. So they pretended it didn't exist.

The machine isn't broken. It's working exactly as designed. It's just not designed to help you. It's designed to extract money from you.

THE HEARSAY PROBLEM

In a real trial, there are rules about evidence. You can't just walk into court and say "He owes me $32,000" without proving it.

You need receipts. Bank records. Payment histories. Documentation.

And if you're relying on someone else's statement—like an affidavit—the other side has the right to cross-examine that person. To question them. To challenge their credibility.

That's called the hearsay rule. It's a fundamental protection in our legal system.

But in child support hearings, those rules don't apply.

Charlotte didn't have to prove I owed $32,000. She just had to say it. In a messy, handwritten affidavit with no supporting documentation.

I didn't get to cross-examine her. I didn't get to ask her about the $10,150 dismissal she had consented to. I didn't get to question why her 2006 affidavit said $5,600 but her 2013 affidavit said $32,000.

The hearing officer just accepted her word as truth.

That's the hearsay problem. In child support court, the rules that protect you in every other legal proceeding don't exist. The State can rely on unverified affidavits, contradictory audits, and fabricated numbers—and you have no way to challenge them.

Unless you know how to force them on the record.

Jess picked up the second audit. "She sent me this."

Different start date. Different total. No explanation. No apology. No acknowledgment that the first audit had been wrong.

HOW TO USE CONTRADICTORY AUDITS IN COURT

If you're in a similar situation—if you've received multiple audits with different numbers—here's how to use them in court:

1. Keep every version.

Don't throw away the "wrong" audits. Keep all of them. They're evidence of fraud.

2. Organize them chronologically.

Create a chart showing when each audit was sent, what it claimed, and how it differed from the others.

3. Attach them to your motion as exhibits.

Label them Exhibit A, Exhibit B, Exhibit C, etc. Make it easy for the judge to see them side by side.

4. Force the State to explain the contradictions.

In your motion, write something like this:

"The State has sent me five different audits, all claiming to be the official record of my child support history. Audit #1 claims I owe $30,000 with a start date of 1992. Audit #2 claims I owe $28,000 with a start date of 1995. Audit #3 claims I owe $32,000 with a start date of 2005. Audits #4 and #5 claim I owe $30,231.90 with a start date of 2006. All five audits are attached as exhibits. I respectfully ask the Court: Which audit is correct? If the State cannot provide a consistent, accurate record of my alleged arrears, how can they meet their burden of proof?"

5. Argue that contradictory evidence is insufficient.

The State has the burden of proving you owe the money. If they can't even agree on how much you owe or when the obligation started, they haven't met that burden.

In legal terms, this is called "insufficient evidence." You're not asking the judge to believe your version over theirs. You're pointing out that they don't have a coherent version to begin with.

MY ADVICE

If you're fighting a child support case—or any legal case—and you can't afford a lawyer, use AI.

Not as a replacement for legal advice. But as a tool to educate yourself, organize your evidence, and draft your documents.

Here's how:

1. Start with questions. Ask the AI to explain legal concepts in plain English. Don't be afraid to ask "dumb" questions. There are no dumb questions when you're learning.

2. Provide context. The more details you give the AI about your situation, the better it can help you. Don't just ask "How do I file a motion?" Ask "How do I file a motion to vacate a child support judgment based on fraud in Louisiana?"

3. Review everything. Don't blindly trust what the AI generates. Read it. Understand it. Make sure it's accurate for your situation.

4. Refine and personalize. AI can give you a template, but you need to make it yours. Add your voice. Add your details. Make it authentic.

5. Use it as a coach. Think of AI as a legal coach who's available 24/7 to answer questions, review your work, and help you strategize.

6. Combine it with other resources. AI is powerful, but it's not the only tool. Read books like "Represent Yourself in Court." Research your state's statutes. Talk to other self-represented litigants in online forums.

AI won't win your case for you. But it will give you the tools to win it yourself.

And in a system designed to make you lose, that's everything.
